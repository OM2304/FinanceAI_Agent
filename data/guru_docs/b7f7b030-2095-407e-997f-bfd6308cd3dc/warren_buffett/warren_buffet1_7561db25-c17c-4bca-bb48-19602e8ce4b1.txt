1. Investment Principles
Buffett’s core philosophy, developed with his mentor Benjamin Graham, focuses on treating stocks as ownership in a real business, not just ticker symbols.

Circle of Competence: Invest only in industries and businesses you genuinely understand. Avoid chasing trends outside your knowledge base.
The Economic Moat: Look for companies with a durable competitive advantage (e.g., strong brands like Coca-Cola, high switching costs, or cost advantages) that protects profits from competitors.
Margin of Safety: Only buy when the market price is significantly below the company’s intrinsic value. This protects against miscalculations or market shocks.
Long-Term Horizon: Buffett’s "favourite holding period is forever." He relies on the power of compound interest, which requires decades, not months, to be effective.

2. Business & Management Tenets
When evaluating a company, Buffett looks for specific internal characteristics: 
Management Quality: He seeks leaders with integrity, intelligence, and energy. If integrity is missing, the other two will "kill you".
Simple & Predictable: He prefers "wonderful companies" with consistent operating histories over complex or rapidly changing industries.
Rational Capital Allocation: Management should reinvest profits only if they can generate high returns; otherwise, they should return money to shareholders through dividends or buybacks.

3. Financial Metrics to Watch
Return on Equity (ROE): Buffett prioritizes ROE (Net Income / Shareholder’s Equity) over earnings per share to measure how efficiently management uses capital.
Debt Levels: He prefers companies with low debt, as high leverage can lead to volatile earnings and increased risk during downturns.
Profit Margins: Look for high and increasing profit margins, which indicate efficient management and strong pricing power.

4. Personal & Life Philosophy
Invest in Yourself: Buffett believes the best investment you can make is in your own skills and education, as it's the only asset that cannot be taken away or taxed.
Emotional Discipline: "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful." Success depends more on temperament than IQ.
The 90/10 Rule for Average Investors: For those who aren't professional stock pickers, he recommends putting 90% of assets in a low-cost S&P 500 index fund and 10% in short-term government bonds.
The 8+8+8 Rule: A leadership principle that suggests balancing life into 8 hours of deep work, 8 hours of sleep, and 8 hours of personal recharge/development.
